Downsizing, rightsized.

The financial-planning move nobody talks about. When it works, when it doesn't, and the numbers that make the decision easier.

For many South Africans approaching retirement, the single largest asset is the family home. Often by a comfortable margin. Which means: for many people, the cleanest way to close a retirement gap is to turn some of that house into retirement income. Nobody likes talking about this, so almost nobody thinks about it early enough to do it well.

The financial case.

A R4 million home sold, replaced with a R2 million smaller home, produces roughly R1.8 million of investable capital (after transfer duty, agent fees and CGT). Invested conservatively at a 5% real return, that's roughly R90,000 a year of sustainable retirement income. For many households, that's the difference between a comfortable retirement and a tight one.

It also lowers ongoing costs: rates and taxes, maintenance, insurance, utilities. A smaller home is cheaper to run, often by R5,000-10,000 a month. That's retirement income you don't need to generate in the first place.

The emotional case, honestly.

This is where most downsizing plans break down. The family home carries decades of memory. Moving away from it feels like a loss even when the maths is clearly on the right side. That's a legitimate feeling, not to be argued away.

Some people find that a planned move, early enough, to a home they actively want (a smaller place in a nicer area, a flat near grandchildren, a secure estate) is a net upgrade. Others find that staying put, even at a financial cost, is worth it. Both answers are valid. The point is to make the decision on purpose, not because nobody ever raised it.

When it works best.

  • Five to ten years before retirement, while you still have the energy for a move.
  • When the new home is a definite improvement in lifestyle, not just a step down.
  • When the freed capital is invested (not spent on lifestyle in the first year).

When done well, downsizing is often the single largest retirement lever available to people who didn't save enough in their thirties. It's worth thinking about before anyone forces the issue.
