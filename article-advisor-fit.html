Finding an advisor who fits you.

Five questions worth asking. And what the answers usually tell you about fit, style, and approach.

The best financial advisors earn their fees many times over. The worst drift alongside your money, taking a small cut of it every year without adding much. Telling the difference is less about credentials and more about fit. Credentials are table stakes; fit is what actually determines whether the relationship helps you.

Five questions, and what to listen for.

1. How do you get paid, exactly? You want a clear, simple answer. Fee-only is easiest to understand. Commission-based isn't automatically bad, but it demands more diligence on your part. Vague answers are a signal.

2. Who owns the products you recommend? Truly independent advisors can recommend anyone's products. Tied advisors can only recommend their own parent company's range. Both can be honest. But you need to know which you're dealing with, because it affects what they can suggest.

3. What does a normal year of working with you look like? How often they meet with clients, how they communicate between meetings, what their review process is. Tests whether the relationship matches the kind of involvement you want.

4. Tell me about a time you disagreed with a client's plan. Good advisors push back gently when they think you're making a mistake. Ones who only ever agree are salespeople. The answer will tell you which kind this is.

5. What happens if I need to leave? Exit fees, paperwork, trail commissions. Good relationships don't punish you for testing them.

The meta-signal.

The single most reliable signal is how they handle your questions. Advisors who welcome tough questions, answer them directly, and respect you for asking, are almost always worth working with. Advisors who flinch, deflect, or hurry past are telling you something important about how the relationship will run.

Fit compounds over years. A slightly uncomfortable hour at the start saves you a lot of difficulty later.
