When to switch advisors, and how.

Not every relationship is forever. Signs it's time to move on, and how to do it without drama.

The advisor relationship you start at 35 isn't necessarily the one that serves you best at 55. Life gets more complicated; portfolios grow; what you need from an advisor changes. Knowing when to make a change, and how, keeps your retirement plan moving rather than stalled.

Signs it might be time.

  • You can't remember the last time you spoke. For most people, once a year is a minimum healthy cadence for an advisor relationship. Less than that usually means benign neglect on one side or the other.
  • They can't explain your portfolio in plain English. If every review leaves you more confused than when you started, the communication is broken and the gap is growing.
  • Fees are opaque or have quietly crept up. You should be able to know your all-in cost without having to dig.
  • Your situation has outgrown their expertise. A good retail advisor might not be the right person for a business owner with a trust and offshore interests. No shame; different problem.
  • They push back when you ask hard questions. The best advisors welcome scrutiny. If yours doesn't, there's a reason.

How to do it cleanly.

Switching advisors is administratively simpler than it feels emotionally. The new advisor generally handles most of the paperwork. You typically don't need to sell and rebuy investments (most products can be transferred in kind).

The order of operations:

  1. Find the new advisor first. Don't leave before you have somewhere to land.
  2. Have an honest conversation with the current one. A short, clear note is fine: "We appreciate what you've done. We're making a change." Professionals handle this normally.
  3. Sign the new mandate. The new advisor's back office moves things across, usually in a few weeks.
  4. Check that trail commissions stop. This is the detail that most often slips through.

The emotional part.

A long relationship can feel awkward to end, even when it's clearly the right call. That awkwardness is normal. It doesn't mean the decision is wrong. Staying with a poor-fit advisor for the sake of avoiding one uncomfortable conversation can cost years of progress. The conversation's shorter than you think; the compounding cost of not having it is longer than you think.
